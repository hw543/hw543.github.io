She looked at the half-restored painting. "I'm in the middle of working on the Diana, Mr. Director."

"That can wait. This opportunity cannot."

Elena suppressed a sigh. In her profession, she had learned that time was relative. For her, minutes sometimes meant the difference between preserved and destroyed history. For administrators like Schumann, appointments and networking conversations were more important.

"All right, thirty minutes."

She hung up and began methodically securing her workspace. The painting was carefully covered, instruments cleaned, and chemicals sealed. Elena removed her white lab coat and examined her reflection in the window pane. Her reddish-brown hair was bound in a strict bun, her clothing -- simple black pants and a gray silk blouse -- was practical and understated. The only extravagance she allowed herself was an antique silver filigree brooch -- an heirloom from her grandmother and a subtle nod to her connection with the past.

On her way to the director's office, Elena passed through the museum's public galleries. Tourists and art lovers strolled among the masterpieces, taking selfies in front of paintings or listening attentively to museum guides' explanations. All these people saw only the polished surface -- the completed restoration process, the refined presentation. The painstaking work that she and her colleagues performed behind the scenes remained invisible. That was exactly how Elena wanted it. She preferred working in the background, immersing herself in the intimacy of artworks, learning their secrets. She gladly left the spotlight moments to others.

Schumann's office was located in the museum's historic wing, a high-ceilinged room paneled in dark wood with a view of the museum park. When Elena entered after a brief knock, the director hastily rose from his desk. "Ms. Winters! Come in, come in."

Schumann was a corpulent man with thinning hair and a preference for expensive, tailored suits. Unlike the stereotypical absent-minded museum director, he was a sharp administrator and brilliant fundraiser -- qualities the museum desperately needed to survive in times of tight public budgets.

A second man stood at the window, his back turned to her. He slowly turned around as Elena entered the room -- tall, slim, in a perfectly fitted dark gray suit that even put Schumann's wardrobe to shame. Elena estimated him to be in his mid to late thirties. His face was sharply cut, with high cheekbones and a straight nose that reminded her of classical sculptures. His hair, deep black and slightly grayed at the temples, was short and precisely cut. But it was his eyes that captured Elena's attention -- dark, penetrating, and with an intensity that involuntarily reminded her of portraits by old Renaissance masters.

"Ms. Winters," Schumann began, his voice a shade too enthusiastic, "may I introduce: Marcus Castellano."

The man stepped forward and extended his hand to Elena. His handshake was firm and dry, his skin cool. "It is a pleasure to meet you, Ms. Winters," he said with a voice that sounded surprisingly warm, in contrast to his cool appearance. A slight accent could be heard -- Mediterranean, perhaps Italian or Spanish.

"Mr. Castellano is the owner of the Castellano Private Museum," Schumann explained. "One of the most significant private art collections in Europe."

Elena nodded briefly. The name Castellano was familiar to her, albeit only marginally. An old European family, art collectors for generations, their private museum legendary but notoriously difficult to access for the public.

"Please, let's sit," Schumann invited them, gesturing to the seating area in the corner of the office. Elena took a seat in one of the leather armchairs, Castellano across from her. Schumann positioned himself in a third chair, slightly apart, as if he wanted to deliberately observe the interaction between his guests.

"Mr. Castellano has a fascinating offer for you," the director began.

Castellano leaned forward slightly. "I have followed your work on the collection of Flemish masters, Ms. Winters. Particularly your restoration of the lost details in Van Dyck's 'Lady with an Ermine' was remarkable."

Elena raised an eyebrow in surprise. The Van Dyck restoration had been a complex project she had completed two years ago. It had attracted some attention in professional circles, but she wouldn't have expected someone outside those circles to have taken notice.

"Thank you," she replied neutrally. "It was a challenging project."

"It was not just challenging. You brought back something that was thought to be lost," Castellano said. His eyes rested steadily on her, as if searching for something in her face. "This ability to reveal hidden layers without destroying the essential -- that is a rare gift."

Elena felt a slight discomfort. She wasn't used to being observed so directly, much less being praised so explicitly. Her work spoke for itself; she didn't need verbal accolades.

"What is this about, Mr. Castellano?"

A fleeting smile crossed his face, as if he had expected exactly this direct reaction. "My private museum houses an extensive collection that has been in my family for generations," he began. "The building itself is a historic jewel -- an early 19th-century city palace with elements from various eras. In recent years, I have had it comprehensively renovated, but..." He paused briefly. "There are areas that require special expertise."

"What kind of areas?" Elena asked.

"Among other things, a fresco cycle from the late 18th century located in the main hall. It is in a worrying condition -- partially overpainted, partially damaged by previous improper restoration attempts."

Elena nodded slowly. Frescoes were a particular challenge -- the connection between pigment and plaster made them more susceptible to environmental influences than canvas paintings.

"Additionally," Castellano continued, "there is a collection of paintings and sculptures that also need attention. Some have never been properly restored, others have been treated by less talented hands than yours."

Schumann cleared his throat. "Mr. Castellano would like to engage you for an exclusive project, Elena. Three months at the Castellano Museum."

Elena frowned. "Three months? That's a long time to be away from here."

"The museum would grant you paid special leave," Schumann explained hastily. "Mr. Castellano has made an extremely generous offer to the institution in exchange for your services."

She understood immediately. The museum needed money, and Castellano was willing to pay a lot. Schumann expected her to agree -- not as a request, but as a barely veiled instruction.

"I already have several projects in progress," she objected. "The Diana, the Titian cycle, preparation for the autumn exhibition."

"All of that can wait or be taken over by your colleagues," Schumann interrupted her with a false smile. "We shouldn't pass up this opportunity."

Elena knew when a battle was lost. The director had already decided. She turned back to Castellano. "Where exactly is your museum located, Mr. Castellano?"

"On the northern Italian coast, about an hour from Genoa," he replied. "A quiet location, ideal for concentrated work. You would live in the museum itself -- there is a guest wing with comfortable apartments for specialists like you."

Living in the museum. Elena suppressed a frown. She valued the separation between her work and her private space. The idea of being surrounded by foreign artworks twenty-four hours a day was both tempting and disturbing.

"Why me?" she asked suddenly. "There are many qualified restorers, including those with more experience dealing with frescoes."

Castellano regarded her with a look that was both penetrating and inscrutable. "As I said, Ms. Winters -- your ability to reveal the hidden without destroying it is rare. My collection... it requires a special approach."

Something in his tone made Elena prick up her ears. There was more than just professional interest. A personal note, almost an urgency that shimmered through his controlled facade.

"I understand you need time to think," Castellano continued, pulling a slim leather folder from his briefcase. "Here are the project details, information about the museum and your accommodation. As well as the financial terms."

He handed her the folder. Elena took it and opened it briefly. Her gaze fell on the figure at the end of the contract draft -- a fee that almost took her breath away. It was more than she earned in an entire year at the museum.

"That's very generous," she said, trying to hide her surprise.

"Art has its price, Ms. Winters. Expertise as well." Castellano stood up. "I'm staying in Berlin for two more days. I hope to hear from you by then."

Schumann also jumped up, eager as a schoolboy. "Of course she'll agree, won't you, Elena? Such an opportunity..."

Elena stood more slowly, her thoughts already on the logistical challenges that a three-month stay abroad would bring. Her apartment, her commitments here, the ongoing projects.

"I'll look at it and let you know as soon as possible," she said finally.

Castellano nodded as if he had expected nothing else. "Excellent. Director Schumann has my contact details." He shook her hand once more. "I look forward to our collaboration, Ms. Winters."

As his hand touched hers, Elena had a fleeting, irrational thought: This hand knew secrets -- secrets that were older and deeper than the artworks she was supposed to restore.

Nonsense, she scolded herself. Too many hours under the microscope, too much solitude in the quiet studio.

Castellano said goodbye with a slight bow and left the office, followed by an obsequious Schumann who wanted to escort him to the exit.

Elena remained alone, the leather folder in her hand. She went to the window and looked out at the museum park, where the first leaves of autumn were beginning to fall. Three months in Italy, in a private museum, surrounded by unknown artworks.

The professional curiosity in her was aroused, despite her reservations. New challenges, undiscovered masterpieces, the opportunity to work with techniques she had only known theoretically -- all of that was tempting. On the other hand, there was something about Marcus Castellano that she couldn't categorize. An intensity that went beyond the usual interest of a collector. As if he wasn't just seeking her professional skills, but something else, something more personal.

She opened the folder again and leafed through the documents. Glossy photos showed an impressive building, half palace, half fortress, on a cliff overlooking the Mediterranean. More images showed interior rooms -- high ceilings, artfully decorated walls, imposing staircases and galleries full of art treasures. And then the fresco Castellano had spoken of -- a mythological scene, partially faded, partially obviously damaged, but even in this condition of breathtaking beauty and complexity.

A smaller photo fell out from between the pages. Elena picked it up and examined it with a frown. It showed a cordoned-off area of the museum -- a corridor with a heavy, locked door at the end. The door was decorated with elaborate carvings, motifs she couldn't immediately identify. Why was a photo of a locked door in the folder?

"An impressive offer, isn't it?"

Elena startled. Schumann had returned and now stood in the doorway.

"Yes, it is," she replied, quickly putting the photo back among the other documents.

"You will of course accept." It wasn't a question.

"I need to think about it, Mr. Director. There are many factors to consider."

Schumann stepped closer, his jovial manner from the meeting had given way to a more serious expression. "Elena, let me be frank. The museum's financial situation is tight. Castellano's offer -- the donation he's making for your services -- would help us bridge the next two quarters."

Elena sighed inwardly. There it was: institutional pressure. "And if I refuse?"

"Then we would have to consider budget cuts. Possibly in your department as well." His voice was gentle, but the threat unmistakable.

Elena nodded slowly. She wasn't a politician, not a fighter in the museum's administrative battles. Her world was colors and forms, chemical reactions and historical techniques -- not budgets and negotiations.

"I understand," she said curtly.

"Excellent!" Schumann clapped his hands, again the jovial director. "I knew you would be reasonable. Let me know as soon as you've spoken with Castellano, yes?"

He left the office without waiting for her answer.

Elena gathered the documents and slowly walked the long corridor back to her studio. Along the way, she opened the folder again and pulled out the photo with the locked door. Why was it here? An oversight or a message?

She shook her head at her own thoughts. Too many old novels read, too many stories about mysterious art collectors heard. This was a professional offer, nothing more.

And yet... Marcus Castellano's eyes had seen something when he looked at her. Something that went beyond her professional abilities.

Arriving at her studio, Elena placed the folder on her desk and returned to Diana, whose face still patiently awaited liberation from centuries of false overpaintings.

"What would you do?" she murmured as she put her gloves back on. The goddess of the hunt remained silent, her eyes half-hidden under layers of foreign brushstrokes.

Elena picked up her scalpel again and began carefully working around the goddess's eye. Layer by layer she revealed, uncovering the original beneath. As she always did -- methodically, patiently, controlled.

But her thoughts were already elsewhere. At a museum on an Italian cliff. At an enigmatic art collector with penetrating eyes. And at a locked door that seemed to be waiting for something that had long been forgotten.

When she left her studio that evening, she had already made her decision. She took out her phone and dialed the number Castellano had left her.

"Mr. Castellano," she said when he answered. "This is Elena Winters. I accept your offer."

A brief pause followed. "I'm pleased to hear that, Ms. Winters." His voice sounded warm but not surprised, as if he had already expected her decision. "I'll have everything prepared for your arrival."

"However, I have some conditions," Elena added. Her decision might have been made under pressure, but she would at least set her own rules.

"Of course. I'm listening."

"I need complete autonomy in my work. No interference with my methods or schedule."

"Naturally."

"Second, I want comprehensive access to all information about the works to be restored -- provenance, previous owners, earlier restoration attempts, everything."

A tiny pause occurred, barely perceptible, but Elena noticed it nonetheless. "That will be arranged," Castellano said finally.

"And third, I reserve the right to end the project at any time if I find that the work doesn't meet my ethical or professional standards."

This time the pause was longer. "An interesting condition, Ms. Winters. May I ask what concerns you have?"

Elena considered briefly. She didn't want to appear rude, but something about the whole situation made her cautious. "No specific concerns. Just professional care. I prefer clear agreements from the beginning."

"I respect that. Your conditions are accepted."

They discussed the practical details -- travel dates, necessary equipment, insurance matters. Elena noted everything in her precise, methodical style.

"I expect you then in a week," Castellano concluded. "My assistant will send you the travel documents."

"Until then, Mr. Castellano."

"See you soon, Ms. Winters. And prepare yourself. My collection will surprise you."

The connection was severed, and Elena was left with the strange feeling that she had just signed a contract whose fine print she didn't yet know.
