Lena closed her eyes, breathed in his familiar scent. "Just tired," she said, the same lie as before, now almost automatic. "Mm." He stroked gently over her arm, a soothing gesture. "You know you can talk to me, right? About anything."

She nodded, unable to speak. Yes, she knew it, theoretically. But how do you explain to someone that you're distancing yourself from them without knowing why? How do you talk about an emptiness that you don't understand yourself? "I love you," he said quietly, the words a breath against her skin. She swallowed, felt a burning behind her eyelids. "I love you too," she replied, and it wasn't a lie. She loved him, in a way she couldn't quite comprehend. It was just that this love felt different than expected, more complicated, less clear-cut.

Julian kissed her temple, his lips soft and warm. A promise, a question, an invitation. She turned to him, let her hand glide over his chest, felt how his heartbeat accelerated under her touch. This was simple, this physical language that needed no translation. She lost herself in his kisses, in the warmth of his hands on her skin, in the slow dance of their bodies. For a moment the doubts, the questions, the uncertainties disappeared. There were only the two of them, connected in the most intimate way people know.

Later, as Julian slept, his breath deep and even beside her, Lena lay awake, staring at the ceiling. Moonlight fell through a gap in the curtains, drawing silver patterns on the wall. She felt the pleasant exhaustion in her body, the light pulsing where he had touched her. The closeness was there, physically, undeniably. And yet part of her felt distant, detached, as if she were watching herself from a distance.

Was that normal? Was that what happened when the first infatuation wore off and everyday life moved in? Or was it a sign that something was missing, that something wasn't right between them? She turned on her side, looked at Julian's sleeping face. In sleep he looked younger, more vulnerable, the lines of concentration that often furrowed his brow were smoothed.

She remembered the moment when she had discovered that he was J., when she had first connected this face with the words they had exchanged in books for months. The overwhelming joy she had felt, the sudden realization that what she had thought impossible could be real. Where was that joy now? When had it begun to fade, to transform into this strange melancholy, this indefinite longing for something she couldn't name?

Maybe, she thought, while her eyes grew heavy with tiredness, maybe she expected too much. Maybe there was no perfect connection, no seamless fusion of thoughts and feelings. Maybe what she had was enough -- this quiet closeness in the night, these shared moments, this hand that held hers when she was alone. Maybe she just had to learn to accept it, without constantly looking for more.

With this thought she finally drifted into sleep, while the moon slowly wandered across the sky and the night approached its end.
